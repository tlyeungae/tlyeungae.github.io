Working Paper

Risk Vulnerability and Portfolio Choices over the Life Cycle [LINK]
[Slide] (available soon)
Abstract: Focusing on the portfolio effects of risk vulnerability and using the SIPP, a comprehensive panel of U.S. households, this paper identifies that young workers' heightened vulnerability to labor income risk contributes to their hesitation in holding risky assets. This susceptibility results in suboptimal decision-making in the financial market due to the positive correlation between labor income risk and equity returns. Ultimately, this exacerbates income inequality between the rich and the poor, as a two-standard-deviation rise in labor income risk is linked to a 3.86% decrease in the ownership of directly held stocks compared to the sample average. Moreover, I provide supplementary evidence to support the hypothesis that workers with a higher human capital-to-asset value ratio will be more vulnerable. This further underscores the tendency of households to hold more risky assets as they approach retirement, in line with the data but different from the predictions of the standard life-cycle model. The economic significance of my findings also reinforces the importance of insuring human capital in the early stages of workers' careers.
Presented at: USI IdEP Brown Bag (USI, 2023)

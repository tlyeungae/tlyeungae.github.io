Working Paper

Asymmetric Labor Income Uncertainty: Implications for Risk-Taking in Financial Markets [LINK]
[Slide] (available soon)
Abstract: This study investigates the asymmetric effects of labor income risk on asset allocation, contingent on the level of income growth skewness. Skewness acts as a reliable measure for assessing the performance of the labor market at a macro level. This is because negative macroeconomic (or industry-level) shocks, which are undiversifiable, result in a distribution of labor income changes that is skewed to the left, and the opposite occurs with positive shocks. Skewness of labor income growth is also important to understand the effect of income volatility -- the measure of background risk traditionally used in the literature -- on portfolio choice. Using microdata from the SIPP, I find that investors who experience high labor income volatility make different financial investment decisions depending on the skewness. When examining volatility alone, it appears to correlate positively with household stock shares, likely because risk-tolerant individuals opt for jobs with higher risk levels. However, when considering skewness, such as at a skewness level of -1, a one-unit increase in variance has a 46% lower impact on stock holding compared to when skewness is 0. This empirical finding aligns perfectly with theoretical expectations, and challenges the dominant empirical methods used to assess labor income risk. It suggests that the impact of labor income risk on asset allocation is not fully captured by considering only the second moment of labor income growth.
Presented at: USI IdEP Brown Bag (USI, 2023)
